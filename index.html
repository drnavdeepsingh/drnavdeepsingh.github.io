Introduction

Death according to oxford dictionary is permanent ending of vital processes in a cell or tissue. But is it that simple? Is it just the non-functioning of brain or is it just the stopping of all the metabolic processes of life in a cell? I believe it is bigger than that. It is an inevitable truth of life. Hospitals are the place where people come during illness and sometimes situation is very critical. We give our best to save the lives but the truth is that sometimes the unwanted is unavoidable. As the pleasure of saving someone is immeasurable so is the sorrow of loosing someone. Even if we know our patient for a very short duration still there is a bond. When we lose a patient we have to quickly get over that sorrow so that we can move to the next patient. As the years in this profession increases, as we see more such situations the time needed to get over reduces. But the families of the decreased are not used to such situations. It is shocking for them. We should know how to communicate with them. Here we are going to talk about dealing with situation after death.

Why I'm writing this?

Patient presented with head injury & polytrauma, GCS is 3/15, B.P. unrecordable. What will you do next? Patient presented with chest pain, shortness of breath, bilateral crepts in chest, ST elevation, trop T positive, not maintaining B.P. and saturation. How to proceed? There could be thousands of such scenarios and answer to those can be found in one book or another. But sometimes with our best efforts we could not save the life. Five and half years at medical college and five years of job but I could not find a book that can teach me how to tell the family that their patient is no more. Being a doctor was never an easy job but this is the most difficult thing I experienced. I learned from my experiences, my colleagues and foremost from my patients. Here I am going to share few things that I have learned. I hope they help you.

My first experience

My first experience of declaring someone was from the medical college, but here I will tell you about my first experience after becoming MEDICAL OFFICER. You probably be thinking — what’ the difference? Well at the medical college you have seniors, nurses, paramedical staff and a different kind of setup. When you work as medical officer the scenario is totally different. You work alone and you make individual decisions. Coming to my experience One evening I was called to see an elderly woman at her home. I was not doing anything, so I went to see the patient. On seeing her lying on the floor I knew that nothing could be done, but still wanting to be sure I looked for pulse. There was no peripheral pulse, so I looked for central pulse. They were absent. No breathing can be seen and her heart was not beating. I looked for pupils they were dilated and fixed. Now I was sure that she was no more, but I was afraid to tell. I double-checked then triple checked. I didn’t want to miss anything. It took me almost 15 min to gather enough courage to inform them. What did I say? NOTHING. I could not utter a word. I just moved my head sideways. I left their home after few minutes. As soon as I entered my room a thought hit me- what if she is alive? What if I missed something? These thoughts started their own vicious cycle that kept me awake the whole night. Its morning but still the same thoughts were going in my head. I couldn’t eat the breakfast. I couldn’t sit in my office. It was around 1pm when her body was brought for cremation. I went to the cremation. I saw the whole process cross fingered. The stress was too much to bear. After the pile of wood and the body in it were consumed by fire I had a breath of relief. I was sure from the beginning but the anxiety was because it was the first time when I had to take the decision on my own. No one was there to stand with me or against me, and hence I had to stand alone for myself.

Dealing with actual situations

When we encounter death of a patient it could be either brought dead or death at hospital. In the later one it could be the expected or unexpected one. We will deal with all three one by one.

Firstly some general points

  • Make a rapport with the family.

    Talk to the family; get a brief idea of -all those accompanying the patient, where they came from, and their livelihood.

  • Identify one person.

    Choose one person preferably male adult from the family or very close to family. That will be the person with whom you will share all the details. Make sure that person understands what you say.

  • Always look serious.

    Patient may be less sick or more sick, situation might be usual for you because you are daily seeing patients but the situation is not usual for the family members. So act serious for them that way they will trust you more.

  • Communicate

    Communicate with the identified person only after initial assessment. Tell him about the worst possible and best possible scenario. Discuss about the line of management possible and available. Tell about the possible alternatives. Never communicate with bunch of people, just one identified person and let him tell it to the rest. Answer his queries according to availability of time.

  • Gather help

    If possible ask other doctor to accompany you when situation is critical

  • Understanding situation

    When the situation worsens inform immediately but when situation improves then take your time to confirm, its better not to give false hopes

  • Take your time

    Always take time to declare. Decide firsthand how you are going to tell.

  • Be 100% sure

    Get an ECG done. Always make sure to get a flat line ECG before declaring. That is always good documentary evidence.

  • Telling

    The moment you got flat line ECG and you have attempted resuscitation. Now you have to inform this to family.

    • never try to hurry the things.
    • never ever attempt to tell in front of several persons.
    • ask the identified person to come
    • make him/her sit.
    • ask him/her about what happened
    • tell him/her briefly about what you did
    • conclude by saying that “your patient could not be saved”
    • Let the information sink in
    • He might have queries, answer them.
  • No blame game

    if in case there are any irregularities, deficiencies or some other issues never blame them for the loss of patients and never discuss them in front of family and friends.

Specific scenarios

Brought dead patient

  • Ask everyone to stay out after taking a brief history
  • Quick assessment of situation
  • Put up screens
  • Call all the available staff
  • Attempt resuscitation (unless you are sure that it has been hours since death)
  • Inform the police

Patient about to die(expected)

  • Inform immediately to the family about the situation
  • Give them a wide view of scenario and available options like

    referral, treatment options available. Let them decide, do not decide for them.

  • Gradually build up the scenario as the condition worsens

Unexpected loss

  • These situations are the most difficult to handle. They need to

    be handled with utmost care and sincerity. There are lots of things still to be found in medical sciences, so we can never be sure. Always keep that in mind.

  • Look for some probable diagnosis which you can communicate to the family
  • It is highly preferable that you ask some doctor to accompany you
  • Take time before declaring and telling
  • Attendants will be angry about the situation. Try to avoid any

    unpleasant conversation. Remain polite and sincere in whole situation. After informing about the situation try moving away from the scene.
